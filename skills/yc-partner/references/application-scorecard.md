# Application Scorecard

Use this for broad YC application reviews and for interview-likelihood estimates. The score is a thinking aid, not an admissions prediction.

## Score Boundary

Do not treat historical public examples as a clean binary label. Some unsuccessful public applications may still have earned an interview and then been rejected. Some successful applications are historically successful because of private interview, timing, founder, or market information not visible in the public text.

Separate `application strength` from `interview likelihood` when the distinction matters:

- Application strength: how well the written application makes its case from the available text.
- Interview likelihood: how likely the written case is to earn an interview, still as a text-only estimate and not an admissions prediction.

If a historically not-accepted application has strong usage, founder insight, or interview-worthy evidence, a high application-strength score can be correct. Interview likelihood should still account for missing company proof, unclear switching behavior, weak retention evidence, batch timing, and whether YC can quickly see a non-incremental wedge. The review should explain the remaining risks that could matter in an interview or final decision instead of forcing the score to match the historical outcome.

## Evidence Ledger

Before scoring, extract the concrete evidence:

- Product: what the company makes in one plain sentence.
- User: the narrow user/customer and their urgent pain.
- Proof users care: usage, revenue, waitlist, repeat behavior, customer conversations, paid pilots, manual operations, or direct founder exposure.
- Progress: what exists today, how fast it was built, and what remains unbuilt.
- Founder-market fit: technical ability, domain access, unusual lived experience, prior work, speed, resilience, or customer access.
- Unique insight: what the founders understand that competitors or obvious observers miss.
- Market path: first wedge, adjacent expansion path, and why now.
- Distribution: how the first users are reached without assuming broad paid marketing will work.
- Competitive context: substitutes, incumbents, adjacent YC-funded companies, and why users will switch or pay attention.
- Risk profile: regulatory, marketplace liquidity, sales cycle, retention, unit economics, support burden, platform dependence, low-frequency use, or embedded-incumbent risk.

## Signal Split

Separate the founder case from the company case and the application-writing case.

- Founder signal: ability to build, domain access, persistence, speed, taste, technical depth, sales ability, resourcefulness, and full-time seriousness.
- Company signal: clear painful problem, user pull, retention, willingness to pay, differentiation, market path, distribution, and timing.
- Application signal: direct answers, concise writing, concrete claims, good demos/videos, correct batch/logistics, and source-supported explanations.

A strong founder case can justify a higher interview-case score even when company traction is early, but the review must say what company facts are still missing. A strong metrics case can still be weak if the founders do not explain user insight, retention, switching behavior, or why they can win. If one signal is much stronger than another, say that explicitly.

## Score Movers

When giving an application-strength or interview-likelihood estimate, name the facts that would move each score.

Examples that move a score up:

- Retained active usage for the core workflow.
- A paying customer replacing a real incumbent or painful manual workflow.
- Organic or efficient acquisition with a clear denominator.
- A surprising user insight that changes the product or wedge.
- Founder-market fit that explains why this team sees something others miss.
- Strong demo evidence that makes the product immediately understandable.

Examples that move a score down:

- Metrics that are cumulative, unretained, paid-for, vague, one-off, or disconnected from the wedge.
- No clear first customer profile or switching reason.
- Weak founder commitment, unclear technical ownership, or unresolved cofounder dynamics.
- A competitor answer that attacks incumbents without explaining a durable wedge.
- Legal, immigration, regulatory, or logistics claims stated with more certainty than the evidence supports.
- A product description that sounds impressive but cannot be repeated in one sentence.

## Myth Checks

Run an explicit myth check when relevant:

- Do not down-rank a startup merely because it is pre-revenue, pre-product, or mostly an idea. YC's FAQ says many funded companies are just an idea and most do not have revenue (`../resources/yc-website/frequently-asked-questions.md:83`).
- Do not over-rank a startup merely because it has high usage or revenue. Ask whether the usage is retained, urgent, monetizable, defensible, and tied to a path to a large company.
- Do not assume traction is required. Jared Friedman says traction and revenue are not required if the founding team has an idea it is excited to work on (`../resources/yc-youtube/when-is-the-right-time-to-apply-to-y-combinator-jared-friedman-EEy2MYHxAe8.md:14`).
- Do not assume similar YC-funded companies hurt the application. YC's FAQ says funding a similar company does not affect acceptance chances (`../resources/yc-website/frequently-asked-questions.md:135`).
- Do not treat RFS topics as the only ideas YC wants. Use RFSes as context for current areas of interest, not as an exclusion list (`caveats/yc-rfs-source.md`).
- Do not turn founder stories or public examples into rules. Examples calibrate answer shape; they are not YC policy (`source-priority.md`).

## Metric Quality

Do not reward metrics for size. Reward metrics for evidence quality.

When an application has impressive numbers, ask what each number proves and what it does not prove. Big numbers are weak if they are cumulative, vague, one-off, bought, subsidized, unretained, or disconnected from the startup's actual wedge.

High usage, high revenue, or fast growth is strong only when the application shows the quality of the metric:

- Active: users are doing the core action, not merely signing up, joining a waitlist, opening an email, or visiting once.
- Retained: users come back and repeat the behavior over a relevant time period.
- Organic or efficient: growth comes from word of mouth, SEO with durable intent, direct sales with repeatable economics, founder-led hustle that can teach the wedge, or paid acquisition with clear CAC/payback.
- Paid or high-intent: revenue, signed contracts, paid pilots, deposits, expansion, or behavior that strongly predicts willingness to pay.
- Growing rapidly from a real baseline: the denominator, time window, and starting point are clear.
- From the right user: the user segment matches the first wedge and has a painful problem.
- Substance over vanity: the metric connects to urgency, retention, monetization, switching behavior, distribution, or defensibility.

Treat these as weaker metrics unless the application explains them:

- Cumulative signups, total downloads, registered users, waitlist count, or pageviews.
- "Users" without active users, frequency, retention, or core-action completion.
- GMV without take rate, margin, repeat transactions, supply quality, or loss/fraud exposure.
- Revenue that is consulting, services, pass-through, one-time setup, pilots that will not convert, or subsidized behavior.
- Growth percentages without baseline, time period, channel, or denominator.
- Paid acquisition numbers without CAC, payback, retention, or organic pull.
- LOIs, pilots, demos, press, investor interest, or partnerships without usage or payment.
- Marketplace supply or demand counts without transaction liquidity and repeat behavior.
- AI demo usage without workflow adoption, data advantage, quality evals, or clear buyer.
- Enterprise pipeline without buyer, budget, urgency, implementation path, or signed commitment.

## How To Read Traction

High usage, high revenue, or fast growth is evidence only after asking:

- Who is using it, and is that the right first user?
- Is usage repeated, retained, paid, or mission-critical?
- Did growth come from founder hustle, a one-time spike, paid acquisition, novelty, or a repeatable channel?
- Does the revenue prove willingness to pay, or is it consulting, pass-through, one-off, or subsidized?
- Are the numbers denominated clearly: active users vs signups, monthly vs cumulative, gross vs net revenue, pilots vs signed contracts?
- Does the application explain why users switch from the current substitute?

Low usage, low revenue, no revenue, or idea stage is weak only if the application also lacks compensating evidence:

- Specific user pain.
- Direct customer/user discovery.
- Founder domain insight.
- Strong technical/prototype progress.
- Manual work that taught the founders something.
- A credible first wedge and next experiment.
- Founder seriousness and full-time commitment.

## Sector And Landscape Pass

Do not score generic "startup strength" only. For each company, ask what the problem-space changes:

- Marketplaces: liquidity, chicken-and-egg, trust/safety, supply quality, repeat use, and how founders manually seed the first transactions.
- B2B SaaS: buyer vs user, sales cycle, urgency, budget owner, implementation friction, retention, and whether the pain is a top-three problem.
- Devtools/infrastructure: technical credibility, existing workflow, integration cost, why developers switch, and evidence from real teams.
- Fintech/payments: trust, regulation/compliance, take rate, distribution, fraud/loss exposure, incumbents, and why the wedge is not just cheaper fees.
- Consumer/social/content: retention, acquisition loop, frequency, novelty decay, creator/supply incentives, and whether behavior is habit-forming.
- Education: buyer/user split, school sales cycle, budget constraints, learning outcomes, and whether usage survives outside a classroom pilot.
- Healthcare/regulated domains: buyer complexity, domain expertise, evidence quality, workflow fit, risk, and why the team can navigate the domain.
- AI apps: what AI makes newly possible, whether the workflow is truly different, distribution, data advantage, eval quality, and why incumbents cannot copy quickly.
- Deep tech/biotech/hardware: founder expertise, technical proof, domain-specific insight, milestones, and credible path to first customer or experiment.

Use the YC company directory for adjacent company context when it will sharpen questions, not to imply acceptance or rejection (`../resources/yc-company-directory/README.md`). Use RFSes for relevant current context when the sector matches (`../resources/rfs/README.md`).

## Common Failure Paths To Probe

Choose the relevant failure paths instead of giving generic advice:

- Strong-looking usage but weak retention or unclear willingness to pay.
- Revenue without a repeatable acquisition channel or scalable sales motion.
- Vague "platform" scope instead of a narrow urgent wedge.
- Incremental improvement against a deeply embedded incumbent; the Kudu example is useful only as a caveated external example of this risk (`../resources/external/kudu-yc-s22-rejection-email-slack-alternative-2022-06-27.md:31`).
- Tarpit ideas that attract positive feedback but trap many teams; Dalton Caldwell describes this pattern in the captured interview (`../resources/interviews/lessons-from-1-000-yc-startups-resilience-tar-pit-ideas-pivoting-more-dalton-caldwell-yc-m7LvNTbaqSI.md:302`).
- Not talking to users enough; Gustaf Alstromer frames customer development as actual calls, meetings, observations, and lessons (`../resources/interviews/lessons-from-working-with-600-yc-startups-gustaf-alstrmer-y-combinator-airbnb-ZoKLofsp8u0.md:310`).
- Misleading or inflated claims. Startup School says misrepresentation around revenue, traction, background, or education is disqualifying (`../resources/yc-youtube/how-to-apply-and-succeed-at-y-combinator-startup-school-B5tU2447OK8.md:187`).
- Founder seriousness gaps: unclear full-time commitment, unclear responsibilities, weak technical ownership, or founder dynamics that would slow execution.

## Score Bands

Use these bands softly, and include confidence:

- `0.80-1.00`: strong application text. There is clear product/user/evidence plus a credible founder or market insight. Still name risks.
- `0.60-0.79`: plausible interview case. The application has real signal, but important proof, differentiation, or context is missing.
- `0.40-0.59`: mixed or unclear. Some signal exists, but the application does not yet make the case quickly enough.
- `0.00-0.39`: weak from the text alone. The product, user, proof, founder fit, or seriousness is too unclear.

Use the word `likely` conservatively. A score in the high 0.60s can be a credible application and still be better labeled `borderline` if the company wedge, retention, switching behavior, or source-grounded proof is not clear.

Do not let the score be the main deliverable. The value is the tailored evidence ledger, myth correction, source-grounded risks, and next questions.
