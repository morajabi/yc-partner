## Content

Gustaf Alstromer (@gustaf), Oct 13, 2023:

17 years ago, I was sitting with my co-founders Marie and Pelle (and later Michael) in our freezing New York apartment crafting our YC application. The first sentence of our application still makes sense today. But it was as revolutionary then, and it seems obvious today:

“We are creating a mobile social networking application that enables free interoperable mobile instant messaging on your mobile handset.”

Marie sent the email, and in that moment, we had no idea what life-changing decision we had just made. Our startup Heysan didn’t become Whatsapp (Whatsapp became Whatsapp), but joining the winter 2007 batch would shape our lives in almost every aspect. Two of us became US citizens and decided to stay here. We all still work on startups in one way or another. For me personally, the most valuable thing was understanding that taking risks isn’t that risky, and if you want to do something, you just do it - don't wait. And it all started with an email that we sent to w2007@ycombinator.com.

Today, we don’t accept applications over email anymore, but you can apply  http://ycombinator.com/apply. And if you read this after the deadline - you can still apply!

Here is something I wrote for my group in the last batch. I think it applies to founders who are just about to apply just as well:

“One of the best parts of working at YC is that sometimes I get to watch a startup go from nothing to becoming a large company that changes society around us, and something mundane in life suddenly gets infinitely better.

That only happens because founders like yourself are willing to take some risks and get that journey started. Soon, we will do demo day practice, and I vividly remember when Retool practiced their pitch until 3am, and we told David, “You are done - time to go home!“. It was a similar feeling watching Alex and Shuo at Deel - they pivoted literally a few weeks before demo day into what today is Deel and a $12B company. So many of the best YC companies had almost nothing at this point in the batch - it’s easy to forget. And from the outside, it’s easy to imagine investors / other people thinking, “How can THIS company be worth this much or feel so confident when they have almost nothing”.

The trick I’ve learned about being a founder is that it starts with you. If you truly believe in what you are building and how that will change the world, all you have to do is tell the investors with the same level of confidence you feel about this change. And I can tell that a lot of you felt that conviction this week. Becoming good at communicating that conviction will help you hire, sell, fundraise, and convince the rest of the world to believe in what you are building, even though it might not feel like much right now to you.

Good luck, everyone, next week. One of the best pieces of advice I’ve ever got is when you are walking into a meeting you feel nervous about - pretend like you have known them your entire life. Don’t get attached to the outcome - just tell your story.”

Good luck!
