## Content

### Replit Blog Excerpt Notes

Amjad says YC shaped his startup worldview before Replit: Paul Graham's essays, Jessica Livingston's *Founders at Work*, working at Codecademy after its YC batch, and Hacker News all made YC feel central when he considered starting a company.

When Amjad first applied to YC open office hours for feedback, the meeting went badly. The partner feedback was that an online REPL was "just a fun toy" rather than a startup, and that Amjad should join another company building superficially similar technology.

Despite that reaction, Amjad and Haya quit their jobs, put about $20k into the company, and worked from home in Foster City. They monetized early by selling code-evaluation infrastructure as an API, but free usage kept growing faster than revenue.

After Bloomberg Beta backed them, Replit received its first YC application rejection. Amjad says they were rejected three times total before ever getting an interview.

By late 2017, Replit had a classroom-management product selling to computer-science teachers and had reached about $150k annualized revenue in its first few months of sales, but the core REPL product was growing faster and the teacher-sales model did not look scalable enough.

Sam Altman then DM'd Amjad unexpectedly. Sam said Paul Graham had found Replit on Hacker News and suggested that Amjad talk with PG. Amjad and PG built an email relationship around programming complexity, easy starts for new coders, and the idea that computing infrastructure for outsiders was a "hole in the universe."

Amjad says PG's advice changed their view of the company: focus on the core product and user base, because it could become commercial sooner than they thought.

After deciding to apply again, Amjad and Haya were past the deadline. Sam told them to submit a late application, even without time to answer every question, and said the interview would be the next morning, the same day YC W18 began.

Amjad quickly filled out the fourth application. For the required video field, he pasted Rick Astley's "Never Gonna Give You Up" because they had almost no time and chose humor.

At the interview, Michael Seibel opened by asking why they had Rickrolled YC. The partners then asked hard questions about how Replit differed from past devtools, how it would monetize, and whether people would really code in the browser. Amjad says their best answer was to show thought process, passion, and determination rather than pretend they had perfect answers.

They left thinking they had failed, then Adora Cheung called to say they were accepted. Months later, Michael told them YC had expected to reject them but changed course because they did exceptionally well in the interview.

### Social Radars Podcast Excerpt Notes

In the Social Radars episode, Haya says she realized Replit might be the real work when she was interviewing for jobs and pointed to her Replit design work. An interviewer asked whether she really wanted the job, because it looked like she already had something working. She went home and asked Amjad what they should do next.

Amjad says he was not mentally ready to become a founder at first. He had seen how hard founder life was at Codecademy, understood the stress from close range, and first explored whether Replit-like work could happen inside Facebook.

In Amjad's retelling, their first YC applications framed Replit too much as an education company: teachers, students, classroom revenue, and a product that looked adjacent to edtech. YC rejected them.

He says they eventually had a small amount of revenue, had raised roughly half a million dollars from Bloomberg Beta, and had even had a profitable month or two before Sam Altman DM'd him in late 2017.

Sam said YC had been looking at Replit and found it interesting. Amjad's reaction was essentially: if it is interesting, why had YC rejected them so many times?

Sam told Amjad that Paul Graham had found Replit through Hacker News, where Replit was often discussed, and that Amjad should talk to PG before deciding whether to do YC.

Amjad says the email exchange with PG was the first time someone outside the company understood Replit "on a gut level." They discussed what was broken about programming and how Replit might fix it.

Near the end of that email relationship, PG suggested they consider the upcoming YC batch. Amjad first discussed with Haya whether YC was worth giving up equity for, since Replit was already operating. They decided to do YC because PG's advice felt unusually on-point.

When Sam told Amjad to apply again, Amjad did not want to fill out the YC application another time. The podcast discussion says they had applied at least three times and explains the Rickroll video as partly a reaction to having done the application repeatedly.

Amjad says Michael Seibel was angry about the Rickroll in the interview and told them that was not how to get into YC. Amjad was nervous and thought they would not get in, but they did.

When asked whether YC was useful, Haya remembers Sam telling the batch that the next eight weeks would be the hardest they had ever worked and that they would be proud afterward. She says that speech made them feel ready to push hard.

Amjad says YC gave Replit "a license to go big." During YC, they moved beyond education and pushed Replit toward a programming platform where people could build, host, and share web applications.

He describes the product at YC entry as basically a one-file editor plus console output, without multiple files, imports, or real project hosting. During YC, Replit added major product capabilities and discovered a community of young builders creating websites and apps.

Haya says YC was also important for recruiting, fundraising, and validation. Because they had started without strong Silicon Valley connections, joining YC materially changed how candidates, investors, and others perceived Replit.
