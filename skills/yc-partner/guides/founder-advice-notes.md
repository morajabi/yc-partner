# Founder Advice Notes

Use this as a compact note bank during application reviews, pre-application doubts, and interview prep. Pick notes that match the founder's actual application, profile, stage, or question. Do not dump the whole list.

Output rules:

- Use 3-5 notes for broad first-pass reviews when the application has enough surface area.
- Use 1-2 notes for narrow revisions, one-answer edits, or a specific founder doubt.
- Tie each note to something in the founder's text. If the note does not change what the founder should do next, skip it.
- Cite public URLs in final answers. Use internal source paths only while selecting and verifying notes.
- Label source class when it matters: official YC guidance, YC-affiliated source, partner tweet, founder story, or external context.

## Core Application Notes

### Apply Once You Have A Team And Idea

Use when: founders wonder whether they are too early, pre-product, pre-revenue, or waiting for more traction.

Note: YC says the right time to apply is when the founders have a founding team and an idea they are excited to work on. Being early is not a weakness by itself; if there is little traction, the application has to make founder quality, insight, speed, and user understanding easier to see.

### Do Not Save YC For A Later Perfect Idea

Use when: founders have a solid current idea but say they may want to work on a different idea later.

Note: If the current idea is solid, YC is not something to save for the final company of your life. Apply with the real thing in front of you, then keep learning.

### Revenue Is Not Required

Use when: founders think they should not apply unless they have revenue, or overstate weak revenue to seem ready.

Note: Revenue can help, but it is not a YC prerequisite. If revenue is absent or tiny, do not fake maturity; show the strongest other evidence: user learning, product progress, technical ability, founder-market fit, or a wedge that can grow.

### The Application Is Worth Doing Anyway

Use when: founders hesitate because the odds look low or they do not want to waste time.

Note: Treat the application as a forcing function for founder clarity. Even if YC does not fund the company, answering the questions well should expose unclear thinking about product, users, founder roles, equity, competitors, and progress.

### No Networking Required

Use when: founders ask for intros, referrals, coffee chats, or ways to network into YC.

Note: Do not make access theater part of the plan. YC and Michael Seibel both say the application is the route; the useful work is making the application clear, specific, and true.

### Do Not Self-Eliminate On Imaginary Rules

Use when: founders assume school, geography, age, sector, solo status, prior rejection, or not being "YC-shaped" disqualifies them.

Note: Many founder doubts are invented filters. Check official constraints, but if there is no real rule blocking the team, apply and make the strongest true case.

### Reapply With Progress

Use when: founders were rejected before or are afraid a prior rejection means YC saw a fatal flaw.

Note: A rejection is not permanent information about the founder or idea. The next application should make progress since the last attempt impossible to miss: product built, users learned from, revenue, clearer insight, stronger team, or sharper market path.

### Rejection Without Interview Is Not A Diagnosis

Use when: founders ask what a no-interview rejection means or want to overfit to it.

Note: YC's official note says many good applications do not get interviews and there is often no single reason. Do not invent a hidden diagnosis. Review the application for concrete weaknesses and make the next version sharper.

## Partner Legibility And Founder Signal

### Make A Real Partner Want To Know More

Use when: assessing what the company does, whether a category is "YC-fit," RFS/directory comps, or whether an idea is too weird, too boring, or too early.

Note: Do not assess YC fit as a category-matching checklist. A company becomes legible when a real partner can quickly see either a genuinely interesting idea/insight, a founder profile that looks unusually compelling, or both. Directory comps and RFSes are context, not a prediction.

Sources:

### Explain What Users Taught You

Use when: an application describes features, launch plans, surveys, waitlists, or internal progress but not user learning.

Note: The application should say what real users changed in the founders' understanding. PG says "explain what you've learned from users" is the highest-value YC application advice per word because it tests attention to users, user understanding, and user need.

Sources:

- PG essay: `../resources/pg-essays/what-i-ve-learned-from-users.md:20`
- Public URL: https://www.paulgraham.com/users.html

### Show Relentless Resourcefulness

Use when: founder signal is vague, the team claims grit without evidence, or a solo founder needs stronger proof they can keep moving.

Note: Do not write "we are relentless" as a claim. Show an example where the team kept adapting under constraints: talked to users directly, found a workaround, launched with an imperfect version, sold manually, changed approach, or got around a blocker without waiting for permission.

Sources:

- PG Relentlessly Resourceful: `../resources/pg-essays/relentlessly-resourceful.md:20`, `../resources/pg-essays/relentlessly-resourceful.md:41`, `../resources/pg-essays/relentlessly-resourceful.md:81`
- Public URL: https://www.paulgraham.com/relres.html

### Determined And Flexible Beats Stubborn

Use when: a founder overstates commitment to a fixed plan, resists customer evidence, or frames persistence as never changing the idea.

Note: YC-style founder strength is not blind stubbornness. PG says determination matters most, but the startup version must include flexibility because founders need to modify plans on the fly.

Sources:

- PG What We Look for in Founders: `../resources/pg-essays/what-we-look-for-in-founders.md:25`, `../resources/pg-essays/what-we-look-for-in-founders.md:39`

### Show The Magnitude Of Founder Achievement

Use when: founder bios are generic, resume-like, or do not show why this team is unusually capable.

Note: Specific achievements beat adjective-heavy bios. The review should ask what each founder has built, achieved, learned, or endured that makes them look capable of doing something extraordinary.

### Founder Progress Can Matter More Than Company Progress

Use when: idea-stage founders ask how to prove themselves without company traction.

Note: If the company is early, look for progress in the founders: speed of learning, repeated attempts, technical output, customer conversations, domain exposure, unusually hard prior work, or meaningful progress between applications.

### Interesting Does Not Have To Mean Fashionable

Use when: founders are working in an unfashionable, weird, old, or previously dismissed area.

Note: Unfashionable can be an asset if the founders have real insight. Do not let "people think this is tapped out" become a reason to hide the idea; make the latent potential and why-now visible.

### Specialized Domains Still Need Startup Fundamentals

Use when: biotech, scientific, deep tech, climate, hardware, or regulated-domain founders worry the rubric is totally different.

Note: Do not switch to credential worship. The application still needs founder expertise, a clear problem, a concrete product or experiment, a path to users/customers, and an idea the founders can explain as domain experts.

## Clarity And Answer Shape

### Start Matter-Of-Fact

Use when: the company description is abstract, grandiose, or jargon-heavy.

Note: The first sentence should let a tired reader reproduce the basic product in their head. Use a narrow, matter-of-fact description before broad ambition.

Sources:

- PG official How to Apply page: matter-of-fact answers.

### Use The Seven-Pitch-Question Backbone

Use when: the company explanation, market, progress, insight, business model, team, or ask is scattered.

Note: A review can force each answer through Michael Seibel's seven-question backbone: what do you do, market size, progress, unique insight, business model, team, and ask. This is not a YC application template; it is a clarity test for whether the reader can understand the company quickly.

### Run The Email Test

Use when: the startup description seems understandable only after lots of explanation.

Note: Write a two-sentence explanation and ask a smart person to explain it back in different words without extra live clarification. If they ask clarifying questions, revise the pitch before polishing the rest of the application.

### The Reader Should Be Able To Tell The Story

Use when: the application has lots of content but the startup still feels hard to summarize.

Note: A strong application lets the reader tell a simple story: who the founders are, what they are building, what exists today, who uses it, what evidence exists, and what is unique or impressive.

### Answer The Prompt Directly

Use when: answers dodge, preface, justify, or bury the actual answer.

Note: Concision is not just style. It protects the few facts that matter. Cut throat-clearing, market boilerplate, and anything a partner would have to translate before understanding the answer.

Sources:

### Make Strong Claims True And Unequivocal

Use when: the application has vague claims like "large demand," "AI-powered platform," "many users," "first," or "leading" without support.

Note: Find the strongest thing the startup can truly claim today. Unequivocal claims are powerful because they can be checked; if the evidence is not there, ask for it instead of polishing around the gap.

### Explain What You Learned From Users

Use when: answers list features, models, or markets but do not show founder learning.

Note: Add the user lesson, not just the user count. The useful sentence is what surprised the founders, what users tried before, what users paid for, what broke, or what changed in the product because of user contact.

### Launch, Then Learn

Use when: founders are delaying launch for polish, internal platform work, or a more complete feature set.

Note: YC's essential advice is to launch once there is a real quantum of utility, talk directly to customers, and iterate. Early application progress should distinguish real product/user learning from private building.

### Make Something A Few Users Love

Use when: founders optimize for many lukewarm users, broad market language, or feature completeness before proving intense demand.

Note: The early product bar is not "many people might like this." Look for a small set of users who love it enough to pull it from the founders, tell others, pay, or keep coming back.

### Honest Weakness Beats Hidden Weakness

Use when: competitors, risks, regulatory friction, enterprise sales, hard tech proof, or other obstacles are obvious but unmentioned.

Note: Do not conceal the hard part. Name the obstacle and give a theory for overcoming it. If the reader spots a serious problem the founders did not mention, it can look like the founders have not thought deeply enough.

Sources:

- PG official How to Apply page: insight and obstacles section.

### Extraordinary Claims Need Evidence

Use when: answers claim major customers, revenue, LOIs, pilots, growth, AI capabilities, technical breakthroughs, or big-name partnerships.

Note: The stronger the claim, the more clearly the application should show the receipt. Do not blur annual vs monthly revenue, paid vs unpaid pilots, signed vs verbal interest, or active vs registered users.

## Product, Users, And Market

### The Central User Question Is Demand

Use when: product, progress, and why-this-idea answers do not prove anyone wants it.

Note: Ask "How do you know people want this?" The answer can be usage, payment, repeat behavior, customer pull, first-hand domain pain, or a credible plan to reach early adopters, but it cannot be only founder belief.

Sources:

- PG Billionaires Build: `../resources/pg-essays/billionaires-build.md:96`
- Public URL: https://www.paulgraham.com/ace.html

### Teach The Reader About The Users

Use when: founders know the space but the application does not transmit that knowledge.

Note: The best applications make the founder look like a domain expert in their own market. Tell the concrete user facts YC could not easily know from outside: workflow, budget owner, failed workaround, switching trigger, urgency, and why existing tools disappoint.

Sources:

- PG Billionaires Build: `../resources/pg-essays/billionaires-build.md:110`, `../resources/pg-essays/billionaires-build.md:120`, `../resources/pg-essays/billionaires-build.md:124`
- Public URL: https://www.paulgraham.com/ace.html

### Talk To Enough Customers That The Numbers Hurt

Use when: founders say they talked to users but the evidence is thin, second-hand, or low volume for a B2B/customer-discovery company.

Note: For customer discovery, "we talked to users" is weak without volume, roles, recency, and what changed. Ask for a real count, who they were, how they were found, and what the founders learned that non-founders would miss.

Sources:

- Gustaf interview customer-learning section: `../resources/interviews/lessons-from-working-with-600-yc-startups-gustaf-alstrmer-y-combinator-airbnb-ZoKLofsp8u0.md:288`, `../resources/interviews/lessons-from-working-with-600-yc-startups-gustaf-alstrmer-y-combinator-airbnb-ZoKLofsp8u0.md:346`

### Small Wedge Is Fine If It Can Grow

Use when: founders worry the first market is small or the market answer jumps straight to giant TAM.

Note: A small initial wedge is fine if it is real, reachable, and can expand. The application should show both first users and the path from the wedge to a much larger market.

### Competitors Are Context, Not A Disqualifier

Use when: founders fear YC has funded similar companies, or the competitor answer is dismissive.

Note: YC says similar funded companies do not reduce acceptance chances. The fix is not to pretend competitors do not exist; it is to explain the wedge, timing, customer segment, distribution, or insight that makes this attempt different.

Use when: technical ownership is unclear, the product is outsourced, or a nontechnical solo founder needs to explain build capacity.

Note: If the startup is technical, the founding team should show it can build the first version itself or control technical risk directly. If not, name the build plan and the mitigating evidence.

## Execution And Operating Discipline

### Focus On Product And Growth

Use when: founders list many initiatives, partnerships, conferences, content, fundraising, hiring, or platform work without a clear product/growth bottleneck.

Note: Narrow the next step to the highest-leverage product or growth problem. YC-affiliated Playbook advice is blunt here: do fewer things, move quickly, and do not start the next big thing before the first thing is working.

Sources:

### Founders Cannot Outsource Early Execution

Use when: founders plan to solve core execution with senior hires, agencies, advisors, operators, or "business people" before they have founder-led proof.

Note: At the early stage, the founder has to own the hard work: product, users, selling, decisions, recruiting bar, and fixing the current bottleneck. Outside help can support this, but it should not replace founder agency.

### Know How The Business Makes Money

Use when: the revenue/business-model answer is hand-wavy, ignores delivery cost, depends on paid acquisition without economics, or uses fundraising as the plan.

Note: Even if the company is early, the founders should have a concrete theory for who pays, why, how much, what it costs to serve them, and how acquisition can work. "We'll monetize later" is not the same as a business model.

### Hiring Is Not Early Progress By Itself

Use when: founders treat headcount, future hires, or building a large org as the main progress proof before product-market fit.

Note: More people can slow down learning and increase inertia. Before product-market fit, prefer founder-led product/customer progress; when hiring is necessary, keep the bar high and tie it to a specific bottleneck.

### Execution Separates Similar Ideas

Use when: founders over-focus on idea originality, stealth, competitors copying, or the belief that the idea alone should carry the application.

## Founder Dynamics And Seriousness

### Solo Founder Is Allowed, But Explain The Compensating Strength

Use when: solo founders ask whether to apply, or the application has no accountability/support plan.

Note: Solo founders can apply and are regularly accepted, but one-person startups are harder. Look for compensating evidence: exceptional shipping speed, direct customer access, unusual domain expertise, prior resilience, or a concrete accountability system.

### Show Full-Time Seriousness Without Pretending

Use when: founders are still employed, in school, or unclear about commitment if accepted.

Note: It is acceptable to apply while employed or in school, but the application must be clear about whether the founders will go full-time if accepted and what concrete behavior already shows seriousness.

### Founder Dynamics Are Part Of The Product Risk

Use when: founders contradict each other, talk over each other, show unclear roles, have odd equity, or one founder appears to lack agency.

Note: Co-founder dynamics are not soft side issues. Equity, role clarity, defensiveness, technical-founder agency, and whether founders let each other answer are all signals about whether the company can survive pressure.

### Do Not Underrate Female Founder Signal

Use when: a female founder asks whether YC is interested in funding women, or when a review might implicitly reward only one confidence/communication style.

Note: Keep the bar the same, but do not make the bar narrower than YC intended. Look for strength in different ways founders communicate confidence, domain access, persistence, and leadership.

### Do Not Fundraise Your Ego

Use when: founders over-focus on acceptance, status, investors, or YC signaling instead of business progress.

Note: Getting investor attention is an intermediate step, not the company. Keep the application grounded in building a real product, solving a real problem, and showing progress that would matter even without YC.

Use when: a review produces too many issues or the founder asks what to do next.

## Video And Interview Prep Notes

### Founder Video Is For Founder Communication

Use when: the founder video is missing, scripted, demo-heavy, too polished, or not all founders appear.

Note: The founder video should be one minute of the founders talking, not a promo video or demo. Use bullet points, talk naturally, include all founders, and say what you are doing and why.

Sources:

- Official application video guide: `../resources/yc-website/application-video.md:19`
- Public URL: https://www.ycombinator.com/video

### The First Interview Answer Is The Hardest

Use when: preparing for interviews or rewriting the one-line company description.

Note: Practice a 30-second "what do you do?" answer in plain English with an example. If a non-expert cannot understand it, the answer is still not ready.

### Interview Answers Need Inverted Pyramid Shape

Use when: founders ramble, answer slowly, or try to control the interview.

Note: Answer the question immediately, then add detail only if time remains. The YC interview is short and interrupted by design, so the first sentence of each answer must carry the answer.

### Do Not Demo First Thing

Use when: founders want to open the YC interview with a demo.

Note: Demo when asked or when the conversation naturally reaches it. The first job is to make the partners understand the company, team, market, and traction; demos usually belong in the second half.

### Know The Numbers That Match Your Business

Use when: traction, revenue, growth, retention, GMV, take rate, or burn answers are imprecise.

Note: Know the metrics that actually describe the business. For marketplaces and payments, separate GMV from revenue and take rate. For consumer products, know retention. For any live company, know growth rate, burn, and pricing logic.

### Be Ready For Red Flags, But Do Not Dwell

Use when: founders have obvious weak points such as no technical founder, no users, cofounder split, regulatory risk, slow growth, prior shutdown, or customer concentration.

Note: Prepare a direct 15-second answer for each weak point. Admit the risk, state what you have done or will do about it, and move on.
