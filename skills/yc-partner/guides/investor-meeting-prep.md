# Investor Meeting Prep

Use this guide when a founder asks how to prepare for investor meetings, fundraising emails, runway conversations, or seed-round planning. This is not legal, tax, accounting, or securities advice.

## Source Boundary

- Prefer official YC Blog fundraising pages for current public YC guidance.
- Use PG essays and partner posts for communication tactics and investor psychology.
- Keep YC application/interview advice separate from investor-meeting advice. A YC interview is not a fundraising pitch.

## Fundraising Readiness

- Geoff Ralston's seed fundraising guide says investors fund when the idea is compelling, the team can realize it, and the opportunity is real and large; for most founders this requires an idea, product, and some customer adoption (`../resources/yc-website/yc-blog/a-guide-to-seed-fundraising.md:68`).
- Route seed mechanics questions to the official YC Blog guide first, especially how much to raise, financing options, SAFEs, valuation, investor meetings, closing, and documents (`../resources/yc-website/yc-blog/a-guide-to-seed-fundraising.md:22`).
- If the company is not ready to fundraise, use the meeting prep to clarify what proof would make the story fundable: customer adoption, growth, revenue, retention, speed, technical proof, or founder-market fit.

## Meeting Story

- Build the meeting around direct claims: what you do, who needs it, proof users care, why now, why the team, market path, and the ask.
- Michael Seibel's pitch guidance remains useful for investor meetings: clear company sentence, market, progress, unique insight, business model, team, and ask (`../resources/yc-partners/michael-seibel-how-to-pitch-your-company.md:1`).
- PG fundraising essays are useful for truth-based conviction and investor psychology. Use `how-to-convince-investors.md`, `how-to-present-to-investors.md`, `high-resolution-fundraising.md`, and `investor-herd-dynamics.md` to pressure-test whether claims are specific and defensible.

## Avoid Fake Milestones

- Michael Seibel's YC Blog post says a financing round is not the same as success; fundraising should be a byproduct of success, and using rounds as benchmarks can push founders toward optimizing for investors instead of customers (`../resources/yc-website/yc-blog/fundraising-rounds-are-not-milestones.md:28`, `../resources/yc-website/yc-blog/fundraising-rounds-are-not-milestones.md:36`).
- In prep, replace "we are raising X" with the company milestone that money helps reach: retained users, paid customers, revenue, product proof, regulatory milestone, or distribution channel.

## Runway And Burn

- For companies with under one year of runway, start with survival math before investor theater. Dalton's YC note warns against assuming investors will keep funding the company or that a failed fundraise can simply become an acquisition (`../resources/yc-website/yc-blog/advice-for-companies-with-less-than-1-year-of-runway.md:52`).
- Make the founder quantify: cash, net burn, gross burn, runway, revenue, committed spend, headcount, and the exact default-alive/default-dead path.
- If the company is default dead, prep should include burn cuts and revenue actions before or alongside investor outreach (`../resources/yc-website/yc-blog/advice-for-companies-with-less-than-1-year-of-runway.md:70`).

## Investor Question Drills

- What exactly do you do?
- Who urgently needs it?
- What proof says they need it badly?
- What changed recently that makes this possible or urgent?
- Why are you the team to win?
- What is the business model and pricing?
- What is the strongest metric, and what is the denominator?
- What will this round let you prove?
- What must be true for this to become venture-scale?
- What is the biggest risk, and what are you doing this month to reduce it?
- What happens if you cannot raise this round?

## Output Shape

For investor-prep answers, use:

- `Investor story`: 3-5 bullets in meeting order.
- `Numbers to know cold`: the metrics, runway, and asks the founder must know.
- `Likely objections`: 3-6 skeptical questions.
- `Next prep work`: concrete actions before the next meeting.
- `Source trail`: public URLs from the captured source metadata.
